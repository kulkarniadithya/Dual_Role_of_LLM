## 1. Interpretation of the “main conjecture”

### What I did

I interpreted the conjecture as:

> For **all** finite simple graphs (G):
> if (G) is nongraceful, then (L(G)) is graceful.

Then I gave cycles (C_n) with (n \equiv 1,2 \pmod 4) as counterexamples.

### Why this might be flawed (or at least incomplete)

* In your project context, the problem is described as **unsolved**, but the counterexample with cycles is completely standard and trivial if the statement is meant for all graphs.
* That suggests the intended domain might be **more restricted**, e.g.:

  * trees, or
  * unicyclic graphs excluding “bad” cycles, or
  * connected graphs with at least one vertex of degree not equal to 2, etc.

If the conjecture was meant with such a tacit restriction, my “disproof” does **not** address the intended version; it only shows that the naïve global statement fails.

### How to repair / clarify

Add an explicit **scope statement** at the start:

> “In this proof we treat the conjecture in its most literal form: ranging over all finite simple graphs. Under that interpretation, the statement is false, as shown by cycles. However, this does *not* rule out restricted variants (e.g. for trees, non-cycle unicyclic graphs, etc.), which may remain open.”

So the logical core of the counterexample stands, but we must be honest that this may only resolve the **unrestricted** version, not the refined problem you truly care about.

## 2. Use of Lemma 3.1 (Gracefulness of cycles) without detailed justification

### What I did

I stated:

> **Lemma 3.1.** (C_n) is graceful iff (n \equiv 0) or (3 \pmod 4).

and then I just said this is “standard” and took it as given.

### Why this might be incomplete

* For a journal submission, a lemma used in the **central argument** must either:

  * be proven, or
  * be clearly cited to a published source.

* I did neither: I gave no explicit reference (e.g. Rosa’s original paper or Gallian’s survey).

So strictly speaking, the proof is not self-contained and the main step “(C_n) with (n\equiv1,2\pmod4) are nongraceful” is only asserted, not justified.

### How to repair

Two options (both acceptable in a paper):

1. **Cite it properly.** For example:

   > Lemma 3.1 (Rosa).
   > *Reference:* A. Rosa, “On certain valuations of the vertices of a graph,” *Theory of Graphs* (1967).
   > Then: *The proof may be found in [Rosa 1967] or in Gallian’s survey on graph labelings. We recall only the statement.*

   This makes the proof of the conjecture logically correct and properly sourced.

2. **Sketch a proof** (if you want more self-containment), but that’s longer and not really necessary if the lemma is standard.

The main logical argument that uses the lemma (choosing (n\equiv1,2\pmod4) to make both (C_n) and (L(C_n)) nongraceful) is correct once the lemma is accepted.

## 4. Step where I conclude “there exists a graph with (G) and (L(G)) both nongraceful”

### What I did

I chose (G = C_n) with (n \equiv 1) or (2 \pmod 4), appealed to:

* Lemma 3.1 to say (G) is nongraceful, and
* Lemma 2.1 + Lemma 3.1 to say (L(G) \cong C_n) is also nongraceful.

### Why this might be questioned

The **logic itself** is fine. The only vulnerabilities are:

* It depends on Lemma 3.1 being true and properly cited (already addressed).

### How to repair

Strengthen the theorem statement to clarify scope:

> **Theorem.** *In the class of all finite simple graphs, the implication “(G) nongraceful ⇒ (L(G)) graceful” is false; specifically, cycles (C_n) with (n \equiv 1,2\pmod4) are counterexamples.*

and add a remark:

> *Remark.* This leaves open the status of the implication when restricted to, e.g., trees, non-cycle unicyclic graphs, or other graph classes.

This way, the correctness of the counterexample is clear, and we’re transparent about what it does and doesn’t resolve.

### Why this might be misleading

However, natural **restricted** versions of the implication remain open; for instance, one may ask whether the implication holds for trees or for unicyclic graphs other than the bad cycles (C_n) with (n\equiv1,2\pmod4).”

That keeps the logic right and acknowledges that the “unsolved” part may concern a narrower statement.
